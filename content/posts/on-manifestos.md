Fenris recently published a Manifesto. You can read it here: 
[gofenris.com/notes/manifesto](gofenris.com/notes/manifesto)

This was not an easy task for me. I am so grateful to folks who reviewed it
before publishing, and honored that people that I admire and look up to are willing
to spend their time giving feedback.

The accompanying update says: 
> We published it knowing that principles documents have become cheap.
Companies, especially tech companies, have spent years issuing values
statements that they later walked back. If anything, that makes it more
important to us — not less — to say clearly what we believe and be willing
to be held to it.

Although I am the lead author, the Fenris Manifesto is emphatically not
my personal manifesto. I may not publish _that_ soon, but the process of
writing one for Fenris helped me think through both, and I made some
personal discoveries along the way. 

G challenged me to write one, a long time back. He said: "Who do you want
to pick a fight with?" When I was wondering how to tell people about the
work I do, he pushed back. Worry less about a portfolio, he said, and
instead *say provacative things, such that people want to reach out to
you*. 

I have read many manifestos in the past few months. If you're ever
writing one, and you're like me, you may want to do the same. I read
Manifestos, and Declarations, and Value Statements and Core Principles, and
Tenets, and countless lists that were confidently stated and Capitalized.
I read manifestos for radical open access, for data policy and digital
rights, for development. I read the Communist Manifesto (well, parts of
it). I read really inspiring ones, and ones that I didn't like at all.
There is a collection at the overly ambitious ["1000 Manifestos"
project](https://www.1000manifestos.com), and I browsed them
for fun. 

The first few drafts, the brainstorming and generative efforts, those 
was really fun. I can barely remember that part, it's been so long, but I
remember looking at the list and being excited by it. I pushed myself to be
more provacative, and to be more dangerous. 

The revisions, and the process of picking through them, that was often a
chore. Especially for Fenris, which serves as a separate and collective
entity, and for which a manifesto serves a completely different purpose,
there were interesting challenges. 

A manifesto for Fenris required and will require collaborating and
alignment amongst a group of people. It's not an individual introspection,
it's a shared aspiration. Second, we wanted a manifesto that considers not
only the organizations and teams we want to partner with, our current and
future clients, but also speaks to the kind of consultants we want to work
with, the type of network we want to build. 

Is it worth writing? Sure. If you think it a manifesto is valuable, then it
is self-fulfillingly so. It is an exercise in articulating a gestalt, and in
facilitating and in collaborating. 

At least for me, it's also an excercise in publishing Something Possibly
Very Important, and accepting it as good enough, even though you think it
still needs work and that we might need to start from scratch, y'all.
