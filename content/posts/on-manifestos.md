Fenris recently published a Manifesto. You can read it here: 
[gofenris.com/notes/manifesto](https://gofenris.com/notes/manifesto)

G challenged me to write a manifesto, a long time back. I had been
wondering how to tell people about the work that I do, and he pushed back.
Worry less about a portfolio, he said, and instead *say provocative things,
such that people want to reach out to you*. He had been talking about
a personal manifesto, not one for Fenris, but the idea got a foothold. 

But it wasn't always clear that it is a good idea. It still isn't clear.
There are good reasons to be dismissive of the idea. On the Fenris
blog, we wrote: 

> We published it knowing that principles documents have become cheap.
Companies, especially tech companies, have spent years issuing values
statements that they later walked back...

In today's world of value-signalling and value-washing, such talk doesn't
mean much. One could probably have an LLM write a manifesto or value
statement in a few moments. Does this mean it is pointless to write one? 

Fenris is not a public company, or a large one, and no one is looking over
our collective shoulder if we were to write a statement and not live up to
it. Does that make it meaningless to publish it?

I don't believe so. 

> ... If anything, that makes it more important to us to say clearly what
we believe and be willing to be held to it.

It's the process of writing it that was most important. In that sense, I'm
not embarrassed that it took me a few years to get it out. 

So what was that process like? 

I confess that I have now read a whole lot of manifestos. I read
Manifestos, and Declarations, and Value Statements and Core Principles, and
Tenets, and countless listicles that were confidently stated and
Capitalized. I read manifestos for radical open access, for data policy and
digital rights, for development. I read the Communist Manifesto (well,
parts of it). I read really inspiring ones, and ones that I didn't like at
all. There is a small collection at the endearingly ambitious ["1000
Manifestos" project](https://www.1000manifestos.com), and I browsed them
for fun. 

The first few drafts, the brainstorming and generative efforts, those were
fun. I can barely remember that part, but I remember looking at the list
and being excited by it. I pushed myself to be more provocative.

The revisions, and the process of picking through them, that was often a
chore. Especially for Fenris, which serves as a separate and collective
entity, and for which a manifesto serves a completely different purpose,
there were interesting challenges. 

A manifesto for Fenris required and will require collaborating and
alignment amongst a group of people. It's not an individual introspection,
it's a shared aspiration. Second, we wanted a manifesto that considers not
only the organizations and teams we want to partner with, our current and
future clients, but also speaks to the kind of consultants we want to work
with, the type of network we want to build. 

G, the aforementioned instigator of this whole thing, had prompted: "Who do
you want to pick a fight with?" Well, sometimes it feels like through all 
those revisions and discussions, we lost some of its teeth. The intention 
was sometimes to be more understandable to an audience, to remove the jargon, 
but in the process the sharp edges got sanded down a bit. Room for
improvement, in _my_ opinion. 

Separately from the contributors, I am so grateful to folks who reviewed it
before publishing, and honored that people that I admire and look up to are
willing to spend their time giving feedback. These are folks I often turn
to, the people on my [Wall Of Thanks](/wall-of-thanks). 

So in the end, it was a whole thing. Was it worth writing? Yes, in my
opinion. 

Would I recommend it to others? Sure. If you believe a manifesto is
valuable, then it is self-fulfillingly valuable. It is an exercise in
articulating values that we too-rarely take time to talk about, in
facilitating and collaborating and aligning with people you respect. 

At least for me, it's also an exercise in publishing Something Possibly
Very Important, and accepting it as good enough, even though I think it
still needs work and that we might need to start from scratch, y'all. 

Also worth noting: although I am the lead author, the Fenris Manifesto is
emphatically not my personal manifesto. This process did lead me to think
about what a personal manifesto would look like, and I think I made some
personal discoveries along the way. But no immediate plans to work on one.
